# Proof Hierarchy

Proof beats promise. Every competitor in your niche can make the same claims. Nobody can fake your results. The strongest ads don't lead with what you offer -- they lead with what happened to someone who bought it.

---

## The Proof Checklist

Before running any social proof ad, score it against these factors. The more you tick, the more believable the ad becomes.

### Delivery
- [ ] Raw and unedited over polished (shaky phone beats studio lighting)
- [ ] Live or in-the-moment over scripted and recorded after the fact
- [ ] In-person capture over remote/virtual

### Voice
- [ ] Customer speaking in their own words, not yours
- [ ] Third-party voice (customer, reviewer, journalist) over founder voice
- [ ] Multiple voices over a single testimonial

### Specificity
- [ ] Specific numbers over vague claims ("lost 14kg in 8 weeks" beats "transformed their body")
- [ ] Named person over anonymous ("Sarah from Belfast" beats "one of our clients")
- [ ] Dated results over undated ("March 2026" beats "recently")
- [ ] Show the result visually, don't just describe it

### Relatability
- [ ] Customer looks and sounds like the target audience (age, gender, location, situation)
- [ ] Customer started from a relatable place ("I hadn't trained in 10 years" beats "as a former athlete")

### Verification
- [ ] Third-party proof (Google reviews, press mentions, awards, certifications)
- [ ] Platform-native proof (screenshot of review, not a quote graphic you made)
- [ ] Volume of proof (10 testimonials beat 1, even if the 1 is better)

---

## Claim Your Proof

Most people write the claim first, then scramble to find proof. Flip it.

1. Collect all the results and data you already have
2. Look at what the data actually says
3. Only make claims the data supports

If your data says "83% of members attend 3+ sessions per week after 30 days" -- that's your claim. Don't inflate it to "transform your life." The specific, provable number is more persuasive than the big promise.

## Proof-Driven Ad Formats

These formats put proof front and center:

| Format | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Single Testimonial** | One customer tells their story. Raw video, minimal editing. |
| **Compilation** | 3-5 short customer clips cut together. Volume of proof. |
| **Before/During/After** | Same customer at three points in their journey. Shows progression. |
| **Group Shot** | Multiple customers together, each with their specific result. Visual volume. |
| **Screenshot Wall** | Grid of real reviews, DMs, or messages. No design -- just proof. |
| **Reaction Capture** | Film the moment a customer first sees their result. Raw emotion. |

---

## Testimonial Interview Questions

Generic testimonials are useless. "It was great, I loved it" doesn't sell anything. Use these questions to extract specific, emotional, personal proof:

1. What was going on in your life when you decided to reach out?
2. What had you already tried that didn't work?
3. What was the moment you knew this was different?
4. What specific result surprised you the most?
5. What can you do now that you couldn't do before?
6. What would you say to someone who's on the fence?

The goal is to get them talking about their **situation before**, the **turning point**, and the **specific after**. Not how great your service is.
